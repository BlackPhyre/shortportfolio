How AI Decides What You Pay for Your Flight
Travel TechSpec Sample~ 4 min read

You check a flight. Close the tab. Come back an hour later. The price has changed.

It feels random. It isn't.

Behind every ticket price is a system built to do one thing: match the right price to the right traveler at the right moment. Today, that system runs on AI.

Why Prices Change So Often

Airlines don't set one price and leave it. They manage thousands of flights across hundreds of routes at the same time. Each seat has a different value depending on who wants it, when they book, and how many seats are left.

Before AI, pricing teams did this manually. They looked at historical data, built forecasts, and updated fares every few days. It worked up to a point. But it was slow and easy to get wrong.

Before vs After AI

Before: Analysts updated fares by hand. Changes took days. Models were based on averages and couldn't react when demand shifted fast.

With AI: Prices update in real time. The system monitors demand signals, competitor fares, booking pace, and seasonal patterns all at once, adjusting automatically, around the clock.

What the AI Actually Looks At

Modern airline pricing software uses machine learning to predict demand and set optimal fares. It monitors booking pace, competitor fares, search activity, events and seasons, and remaining inventory, all at the same time.

What This Means for Airlines

Airlines using AI pricing fill more seats, discount less at the last minute, and earn more per flight. Not by raising prices across the board. By matching fares to demand more accurately.

The next step is full offer personalisation. A business traveler might see a fare bundled with lounge access. A leisure traveler might see a flexible ticket with a hotel option. Same flight, different offer, right price for each person.

That's where the industry is heading. AI is what makes it work.

Why Airlines That Wait on Continuous Pricing Will Pay for It Twice
Travel TechSpec Sample~ 8 min read

Most airlines know continuous pricing works. The ones not using it are not waiting for proof. They are waiting for the right time. This piece is about what that wait is costing them.

Author Note The debate about whether continuous pricing delivers value has been settled by the carriers that adopted it early. The conversation now is about timing. I wrote this for the commercial teams still in that conversation, and for what it is costing them to stay there.
Featured Image
Key Takeaways
  • Delaying continuous pricing carries a compounding revenue cost on every booking cycle.
  • Class-based fare structures create artificial price gaps that demand does not follow.
  • The transition is more modular than most airlines expect. A full system overhaul is not a prerequisite.
  • Early movers build pricing intelligence that compounds over time. Late movers start from behind.
  • The question is no longer whether to adopt. It is how much ground to concede before doing so.

The Cost of Waiting Is Not Zero

Most airline commercial teams that have not adopted continuous pricing understand its value. The business case is not the sticking point.

The sticking point is timing. The belief that the transition is too disruptive right now, that legacy infrastructure is not ready, that the organisation needs more time.

What that reasoning misses is that waiting has a cost. It does not appear as a line item. It does not show up in the monthly P&L. But it is real, and it accumulates on every booking cycle.

Key Point

A traveller who would have booked at €215 abandons the search when the next available fare class jumps to €260. That €45 gap is not a pricing decision. It is a structural artifact of a system built for inventory management, not revenue optimisation. Continuous pricing closes that gap. Every day it stays open is revenue that does not come back.

The Compounding Problem With Class-Based Controls

Fare class architectures were designed for a GDS-driven world where the priority was managing inventory across a fixed set of booking classes.

In a direct distribution environment where airlines construct offers in real time and respond to demand elasticity at the individual transaction level, class-based pricing is a constraint masquerading as a pricing strategy.

An airline with 26 fare classes has 26 price points on any given flight. The market has millions of demand signals. Every search that lands in the gap between two fare classes either converts below what the traveller would have paid, or does not convert at all.

McKinsey estimates modern retailing could unlock $45 billion in new industry value by 2030. Continuous pricing is a foundational part of that figure. The airlines capturing it will not be the ones still deciding whether to move.

The structural gapClass-based pricing creates artificial steps that demand does not follow. Continuous pricing removes those steps. The difference shows up in both conversion and yield.

Why "We Are Not Ready" Is Often the Wrong Frame

The most common reason airlines give for delaying is infrastructure readiness. The PSS does not support it. GDS agreements restrict it. Continuous pricing will be addressed in a later phase of the Offer and Order transformation.

The infrastructure concern is legitimate. Legacy PSS environments were not designed to process dynamic, class-free prices. Some agreements actively create friction for airlines trying to introduce third-party pricing tools.

Important

These are real barriers. They are not a reason to wait for everything to be resolved before moving. The most effective continuous pricing implementations are modular by design. They operate within existing class-based environments, not instead of them.

Airlines that frame this as all-or-nothing tend to delay indefinitely. Airlines that treat it as a modular capability built incrementally tend to have measurable results within months.

The Competitive Asymmetry That Builds Over Time

Continuous pricing systems improve as they process more data. Demand elasticity models become more accurate. Booking curve patterns for specific routes and seasons are learned and refined.

An airline that has been running continuous pricing for two years has two years of elasticity data, booking pace intelligence, and model calibration that a new adopter does not have.

A competitor entering in year three does not start at the same point. They start from where the early mover began two years ago and work forward from there.

What early adoption actually meansThe advantage of moving first is not just the revenue captured in year one. It is the pricing intelligence built over time. Model accuracy, booking pattern recognition, and fare optimisation precision all improve with data. That gap does not close overnight.

The Question Has Already Shifted

The debate about whether continuous pricing delivers value has largely been resolved. Carriers including Lufthansa Group and Air Canada have demonstrated its revenue impact at scale.

The question commercial leaders are now asking is not whether to adopt it. It is when, and how to structure the transition to minimise disruption while accelerating time to value.

Continuous pricing is not the end state of airline retail transformation. It is the foundation that makes everything above it possible: offer optimisation, dynamic ancillary pricing, personalised bundles at the moment of request.

Delay does not buy time. It narrows the window.

FAQ
What is continuous pricing and how does it differ from class-based pricing?
Class-based pricing assigns fares to a fixed number of booking classes, typically 26. Prices move in steps between those classes. Continuous pricing removes the class structure and generates fare points dynamically at the moment of each booking request, based on real-time demand signals and elasticity modelling. The fare follows demand rather than predefined inventory thresholds.
Does continuous pricing require replacing the PSS?
No. The most effective implementations are modular. A continuous pricing layer can operate alongside an existing class-based PSS environment, generating dynamic fare points that supplement rather than replace legacy structures. This allows airlines to capture revenue benefits without waiting for a complete infrastructure overhaul.
How quickly do airlines typically see revenue impact after go-live?
Based on documented airline implementations, measurable revenue improvement typically appears within 8 to 12 weeks of go-live on initial routes. Full-network impact is generally visible by the end of year one, with results that tend to exceed the original business case projection.

Why Most Crypto Platforms Still Price Like It Is 2018
CryptoSpec Sample~ 8 min read

The crypto industry competed on published APY for a decade. As the market matures, that model is running out of road.

Author Note Flat-rate yield models were the right tool for a market that needed to attract users fast and had no established benchmark for fair value. That market is gone. This piece is about what replaces it, and why platforms that move first will be harder to displace.
Featured Image
Key Takeaways
  • Flat-rate yield and lending models were designed for an early-stage acquisition market. That market no longer defines the landscape.
  • User behaviour, collateral quality, and market conditions vary enough to justify dynamic, differentiated rate structures.
  • Platforms that personalise rates based on risk and engagement retain more assets and produce better margin.
  • The infrastructure for dynamic crypto rate management exists. Most platforms have not built the commercial logic to use it.
  • Static rates have a compounding revenue cost as the asset base scales.

The Rate That Does Not Move

In 2018, a 10% APY on a Bitcoin deposit was designed to attract attention in a market that had no reference for fair value. It worked.

The problem is that many platforms are still pricing with the same logic. A published rate. A fixed structure. A model built for user acquisition that was never redesigned for retention, margin efficiency, or risk-adjusted yield management.

The crypto market has changed significantly since then. Institutional participation has increased. Regulatory frameworks are being established. User sophistication has risen. And yet yield and lending rate models at most platforms remain structurally similar to what they were when the priority was growth at any margin.

That is not a sustainable position for a maturing asset class.

What Static Rates Actually Cost

A flat rate applied uniformly treats all assets and all users as equivalent. They are not.

A long-term holder with a three-year history and a diversified portfolio represents a different risk and retention profile than a new user depositing for the first time ahead of a market peak.

A borrower with a low loan-to-value ratio and a clean repayment history is a different counterparty than one approaching their liquidation threshold.

Key Point

Pricing them identically is not fairness. It is an analytical gap with a revenue cost attached. On the yield side, high-value long-term users receive the same rate as marginal ones, compressing margin without producing proportional retention. On the lending side, risk is not being priced into the rate.

User ProfileStatic Rate ModelDynamic Rate Model
Long-term holder, 3+ yearsSame APY as all other usersLoyalty-adjusted rate reflecting tenure
New user, first depositSame APY as long-term usersStandard rate — no unearned premium
Low LTV borrowerStandard rate regardless of riskRisk-adjusted rate — lower cost for lower risk
High LTV borrowerSame rate as low-risk borrowersRate reflects elevated risk — margin protected
Multi-product userEach product priced independentlyRelationship rate reflects full engagement
The core problemStatic rate models generate predictable revenue at the cost of precision. They leave yield on the table for high-value users and underprice risk at the other end of the portfolio. As the asset base scales, both effects compound.

What Dynamic Rate Structures Look Like in Practice

Dynamic pricing in crypto does not mean rates that change by the minute. It means rates calibrated to the actual risk and value of each user relationship rather than a single published number.

For yield products, this means differentiating by tenure, asset type, collateral quality, and platform engagement. A user who has held assets for two years, maintains a diversified portfolio, and uses multiple products is worth more than their deposit balance suggests. Their rate should reflect that.

For lending, this means loan-to-value thresholds and interest rates that respond to collateral volatility, market conditions, and borrower history. When BTC volatility spikes, the risk profile of every BTC-collateralised loan changes. A static rate structure does not account for that.

Important

The technical infrastructure exists at most mature platforms. Risk scoring, on-chain data, transaction history, and market feeds can all feed a rate model. What most platforms have not built is the commercial logic that translates those inputs into differentiated rates at scale. That is a product decision, not an infrastructure one.

The Retention Argument

Beyond margin, dynamic rate structures produce a retention effect that static models cannot replicate.

A user who receives a rate improvement based on tenure or portfolio behaviour has a reason to stay tied to their relationship with the platform, not just to a headline number a competitor can undercut.

Competing on published rates is a race no platform wins sustainably. Platforms that build retention through relationship-based pricing create switching costs that a competitor's APY cannot easily overcome.

The retention premiumA user retained through relationship-based pricing has a lower acquisition cost on the next product. Over a scaling user base, that difference in unit economics adds up.

The Window Is Not Open Indefinitely

As regulation increases and institutional standards for risk-adjusted return become the benchmark, platforms running flat-rate models will face pressure from two directions at once.

Users will expect rates that reflect their value to the platform. Regulators and institutional counterparties will expect pricing that reflects actual risk.

Meeting both expectations with a single published rate is not possible.

The platforms building dynamic rate infrastructure now are not just improving margin. They are building the commercial architecture that the next stage of crypto finance requires. The ones waiting are not holding a neutral position. They are falling behind one that compounds.

FAQ
What is the problem with flat-rate yield models as a platform scales?
Flat-rate models treat all users as equivalent regardless of risk profile, tenure, or platform value. As a user base scales, two problems compound: high-value, low-risk users are over-rewarded relative to the margin they generate, and high-risk borrowers are under-priced, accumulating portfolio exposure without corresponding compensation.
Does dynamic rate management require significant infrastructure changes?
The technical infrastructure already exists at most mature platforms. The gap is in the commercial logic layer: the rules and models that translate risk inputs into differentiated rates at scale. That is a product and data decision, not primarily an infrastructure one.
How does relationship-based pricing improve user retention?
When a user's rate reflects their tenure and platform engagement, their reason to stay is tied to their relationship with the platform rather than a headline number a competitor can undercut. This creates switching costs that rate competition alone cannot produce.
How should platforms approach the transition from static to dynamic rate models?
Start with tenure-based yield differentiation, the lowest friction entry point. Validate the retention and margin impact. Then extend to risk-adjusted lending rates, collateral volatility responses, and cross-product relationship pricing. Attempting to redesign the full rate architecture at once creates operational risk and delays time to value.

Introduction: The Problem with Spending Without Visibility

Most companies do not have a spending problem. They have a visibility problem.

The money going out is accounted for. Invoices paid, budgets approved, quarterly reports filed on time. But knowing where money went is not the same as knowing whether it should have gone there.

That gap between what a company spends and what it actually needs to spend is almost never visible until it is too late to do anything about it.

By the time a finance team spots an issue, the decisions that caused it are months old. The overpaid vendor contract renewed automatically. The duplicate payment sat undetected across two systems. The software licences nobody uses kept getting charged. None of it dramatic on its own. All of it adding up quietly, department by department, quarter by quarter.

This is the problem AI is genuinely good at solving. Not because it is smarter than a finance team, but because it does not get tired, does not miss patterns buried in 200,000 rows of data, and does not need to be asked twice to check the same thing again next month.

This paper is about how that works in practice. Not the pitch version. The real version. What the tools actually do, where they deliver, and what gets in the way when companies try to implement them.

Section 02

What AI Cost Optimisation Actually Means

The phrase gets used in a lot of ways, usually by people trying to sell something. So here is a plain version.

Key PointAI cost optimisation: using machine learning and data analysis to find where money is being spent poorly, and to help make better decisions about it. Not one product. Not one department. A category of tools that applies differently depending on what part of the business you are looking at.

ImportantWhat it is not: a way to cut headcount, call it efficiency, and move on. That approach gets short-term results and longer-term resentment. The companies that see real improvement use AI to make smarter decisions — not to avoid making decisions altogether.

Key distinctionCost-cutting removes resources. Cost optimisation finds where resources are being used poorly and puts them to better use. One shrinks a business. The other makes it sharper.

The tools range from spend analytics platforms to contract intelligence software to AI-assisted procurement systems and dynamic pricing engines. Each one looks at a different slice of the spending picture. Used together, they give companies a level of financial control that was genuinely not possible ten years ago.

Section 03

Where Companies Waste the Most Money

Before looking at how AI helps, it is worth being honest about where the money actually goes. The same patterns show up across industries and company sizes.

  • Tail spend: purchases made outside of approved supplier agreements, usually by individual departments acting independently. Small on their own, significant in aggregate, and the hardest to track.
  • Contract leakage: the difference between what a contract promises and what actually gets invoiced. Discounts expire unclaimed. Volume commitments go untracked. In organisations with hundreds of vendor contracts, this gap is substantial.
  • Duplicate payments: the same invoice paid more than once due to system errors or manual processing mistakes. More common than most finance teams want to admit.
  • Underused software and subscriptions: licences purchased, seats allocated, and tools renewed that are barely used. In companies that have grown quickly, this category alone can represent a significant unnecessary cost.
  • Poor demand forecasting: ordering too much stock, holding excess inventory, or purchasing at the wrong time because the data behind the decision was incomplete or outdated.
  • Uncompetitive supplier pricing: paying more than necessary because procurement teams do not have the time or data to benchmark supplier rates against the market.

Section 04

How AI Finds and Fixes Waste

The mechanism is not magic. AI can process large volumes of data fast, match patterns against known waste signatures, flag what looks wrong, and surface recommendations. The reason it works is scale.

A finance team can review hundreds of invoices a week. An AI system can review hundreds of thousands, without getting bored, without missing the third duplicate of the same invoice, without needing a break.

Spend analysis

AI pulls data from across the business and puts it in one place. For the first time, a company can see the full picture of where money is going. Most finance teams have never had this view. Just having it tends to surface obvious savings before any further analysis is done.

Contract intelligence

Contracts are full of commitments that nobody tracks after signing. Volume thresholds that trigger discounts nobody claims. Renewal dates that pass unnoticed. AI reads the contracts, extracts what matters, and monitors actual spend against the terms. It is the difference between a contract being a legal document and a contract being a live tool.

Anomaly detection

Duplicate invoices have patterns. Billing errors have patterns. AI learns what normal looks like for a specific business and flags what does not fit. This happens in real time, not in a quarterly audit six weeks after the problem occurred.

Demand forecasting

Getting the timing right on purchasing matters more than most companies realise. Buy too much and you carry excess inventory. Buy at the last minute and you pay a premium. AI forecasts what will be needed and when. The savings are not dramatic on any single order. Across a full year of purchasing, they add up.

Worth notingAI does not make the final call on most of these decisions. It identifies, flags, and recommends. A person still decides. Businesses that forget this tend to have problems.
Section 05

Real-World Applications Across Industries

The same tools land differently depending on the industry. Here is where cost optimisation AI shows up in practice, and what it is actually doing in each context.

  • Retail and e-commerce: dynamic pricing tools adjust product prices based on demand, competitor activity, and inventory levels. Procurement AI identifies where consolidated purchasing can reduce unit costs.
  • Healthcare: supply chain AI reduces waste from expired medical inventory, identifies savings in procurement contracts, and flags billing irregularities in large hospital systems where manual review is not feasible.
  • Financial services: AI monitors vendor spend across a complex supplier ecosystem, identifies duplicate contracts from mergers and acquisitions, and benchmarks service costs against market rates.
  • Manufacturing: predictive maintenance tools reduce costs by scheduling equipment servicing before failures occur. Energy optimisation AI reduces consumption by adjusting usage patterns in real time.
  • Travel and aviation: AI revenue management tools set ticket prices dynamically based on demand signals, booking pace, and competitor pricing. The same logic applies to hotel inventory, car rentals, and ancillary services.
  • Technology companies: cloud cost management AI monitors infrastructure spend and identifies unused or oversized resources. SaaS licence management tools flag underused seats and automate renewal decisions.

"The biggest surprise for most clients is not the size of the savings. It is how long the waste had been sitting there undetected."

Common observation from enterprise procurement consultants
Section 06

What Changes When AI Handles Procurement and Pricing

Two areas change the most when AI is properly implemented: procurement and pricing. Both involve high-volume, time-pressured decisions made repeatedly. Both are areas where getting it slightly more right, consistently, produces results that compound over time.

Procurement

Traditional procurement is slow by design. Every stage requires manual effort from people who are already stretched. AI handles the analytical parts faster and more accurately, so the people doing the work can focus on the parts that actually require judgement.

Supplier benchmarking: AI shows what a company is paying compared to what similar companies pay for the same goods or services. Most procurement teams negotiate on feel and history. AI gives them a number. That number changes the conversation.

Supplier risk monitoring: AI watches news, financial filings, and market signals for the suppliers that matter most. A financial difficulty, a regulatory investigation, a logistics disruption. These show up in the data before they become an operational problem.

Pricing

For companies that sell, AI-driven pricing has a direct line to revenue. Not all demand is equal, so not all prices should be either. A fixed pricing model leaves money on the table at peak demand and loses deals during slow periods. AI adjusts for both.

Dynamic pricing: AI responds to demand signals, inventory, competitive activity, and customer behaviour in real time. Airlines figured this out years ago. Hotels followed. The same capability is now accessible to businesses that are not running million-seat inventory systems.

Deal optimisation: in B2B sales, AI analyses what has closed before and tells a sales team what offer is most likely to win without giving away more margin than necessary. Sales teams that use this stop over-discounting to close deals they could have won anyway.

Section 07

What Companies Get Wrong During Implementation

Most AI cost optimisation projects do not fail because the technology does not work. They fail in the months before anyone opens the software.

  • Starting with bad data: AI is only as good as the data it runs on. Companies that feed inconsistent, incomplete, or poorly structured data into an AI system get unreliable outputs. Cleaning and organising data before implementation is not optional. It is the work that determines whether everything else succeeds.
  • Treating it as an IT project: AI cost optimisation affects finance, procurement, operations, and sales. When it is owned by IT and delivered without involvement from the business functions it is meant to serve, adoption is low and results are limited.
  • Expecting results too quickly: most AI systems improve over time as they process more data and receive feedback. Companies that measure ROI at three months and declare the project unsuccessful often abandon tools that would have delivered significant value by month nine or twelve.
  • Skipping change management: people whose jobs involve the decisions AI is now assisting will react to that change. Without clear communication about what the tool does and how it affects their work, resistance builds and adoption stalls.
  • Over-automating too early: moving too fast from AI recommendations to full automation, before the system has been validated, creates risk. The most successful implementations automate gradually, starting with low-stakes decisions and expanding as confidence in the system grows.
Common patternCompanies that spend time on data quality and stakeholder alignment before going live get better results. Not marginally better. Significantly better. The preparation is not a delay. It is the work. Treating it as a box to tick is how projects end up being quietly shut down six months later.
Section 08

Where to Start

The companies that struggle with this tend to start too big. They want to optimise everything at once and end up making slow progress on all of it. Start with one area where the problem is visible and the data is already there.

  • Pick one area with clear waste: spend analysis, contract management, and software licence tracking are all good starting points. They produce visible results quickly and require less change management than pricing or procurement transformation.
  • Audit your data first: before selecting a tool, understand what data you have, where it lives, and how clean it is. This step takes time but prevents a much larger problem later.
  • Set a specific goal: "reduce costs" is not a target. "Reduce tail spend by 15% within 12 months" is. Specific targets make it possible to measure progress and keep the project accountable.
  • Involve the people affected: include finance, procurement, and operations from the start. Their input improves the implementation and their buy-in is essential for adoption.
  • Review and adjust regularly: AI systems improve with feedback. Build in regular review points where the outputs are assessed, corrections are made, and new opportunities are identified.

There is no universal starting point. The right one depends on company size, industry, data quality, and where the biggest inefficiencies actually sit.

What is consistent across every successful implementation is the framing: this is a business change that uses technology, not a technology project that happens to affect the business. The ones that get that right tend to stick with it. The ones that do not tend to produce a case study nobody wants to share.

Conclusion

AI cost optimisation is not new. The tools have been around long enough to have a track record — which means there is real data on what works and what does not, not just vendor promises.

What has changed is access. Capabilities that used to require a dedicated data science team and a seven-figure technology budget are now available through SaaS platforms that mid-sized businesses can afford and deploy.

The companies that move on this build an advantage that is hard to close later. Not because AI is a shortcut, but because financial visibility compounds. Every month of cleaner data, better contracts, and smarter pricing decisions makes the next month easier.

The waste is already there. AI just makes it visible.

The Challenge

Aero Nordic is a mid-size regional carrier operating 34 routes across Northern Europe. By 2023, the revenue management team had identified a pattern the booking data confirmed: peak-demand flights were being systematically undersold while low-load routes were holding fares too high to stimulate demand.

The root cause was not analytical. The team understood what needed to happen. The problem was that their class-based RM system updated fares once a day. In a market where competitor pricing, booking pace, and load factor shift continuously, a 24-hour update cycle means every pricing decision is already stale by the time it reaches the channel.

The commercial team's estimate: pricing lag was costing the network between €10M and €16M annually in unrealised revenue. The case for continuous pricing was clear. The path to get there was not.

The Decision

Aero Nordic evaluated three vendors over six months before selecting a continuous pricing platform built on demand-elasticity modelling and real-time fare optimisation.

The evaluation surfaced tensions that are common in this kind of transition. The commercial team questioned how much pricing autonomy would transfer to the system. The RM analysts, who had spent years building fare intuition across specific route pairs, were sceptical of recommendations that occasionally contradicted their read of the market. The IT team raised legitimate concerns about integrating a modern pricing engine with legacy PSS infrastructure that had been in place for over a decade.

What resolved the decision was an eight-week proof of concept across five routes. The continuous pricing system outperformed class-based controls on four of them. One route showed a 22% improvement in revenue per available seat. The fifth was flat. For a carrier operating on 3-4% margins, that proof was enough.

"We did not want to automate for the sake of it. We needed a system that could act on demand signals our team could see but could not respond to fast enough."

Chief Commercial Officer, Aero Nordic (fictional)

The Implementation

Deployment followed a phased rollout. Eight routes went live in month one. The team monitored bid price behaviour, booking pace response, and revenue outcomes closely before expanding. Full network coverage was reached by month four.

Key PointThe hardest part was not technical integration. It was analyst trust.

RM analysts accustomed to class-based controls found it difficult to accept system recommendations that did not map to familiar fare ladder logic. Two analysts requested manual override capability on specific routes during the first month. That was approved. By month three, neither was using it. The continuous pricing decisions were producing better load and yield outcomes than the override positions they had held.

Before a single fare was touched by the new system, the implementation team spent six weeks auditing and cleaning historical booking data. That work delayed go-live by three weeks. It was also, in the project lead's assessment, the decision that determined whether everything else would work.

The Results

Revenue impact was measurable eleven weeks after the first routes went live. By the end of year one, the full-network picture was clear.

Year one outcomesRevenue per available seat improved 18% across the network. Load factor on peak-demand routes increased by 6 percentage points. Last-minute discounting fell 31% as improved demand forecasting allowed the system to hold fares with confidence closer to departure. The €14M revenue recovery, measured against total platform and implementation cost, delivered ROI the board had not projected until year two.

What Changed and What Did Not

The RM team did not shrink. Their work changed. Manual fare filing and reactive class adjustments gave way to route strategy, competitive positioning, and network-level decisions that require context the system is not designed to carry.

Override capability remains. When a competitor withdraws capacity on a route, when a major event is announced after the booking window opens, or when an operational disruption creates atypical demand patterns, those situations still warrant analyst intervention. The team recognises them. Outside of those moments, they let the system price.

The airline did not become a technology company. It became a carrier with pricing infrastructure that matches the speed of its market.

The Lesson

The pattern Aero Nordic experienced is consistent with how continuous pricing transitions tend to unfold. The technology performs. Data preparation takes longer than planned. Analyst adoption is the real implementation challenge. And year one results typically exceed the original business case.

Airlines still operating on daily fare update cycles are not in a stable position. They are losing ground to carriers whose pricing responds to demand in real time, across every channel, without waiting for the next analyst review.

That gap does not stay the same size. It grows.
